## How to verify a piercing is truly nickel-free?

1. **Look for the ASTM F136 label** on the titanium product page. It's the US reference standard for implant-grade titanium: residual nickel below 0.05%.

2. **For niobium**, require the "pure niobium" or "99.9% niobium" mention. Avoid unspecified niobium-titanium alloys.

3. **For solid gold**, 14 or 18 carat yellow or rose gold is nickel-free by construction (alloyed with copper and silver). Beware of entry-level white gold, often nickel-alloyed — prefer palladium-alloyed white gold.

4. **For gold-plated silver**, require 18-carat vermeil at least 2 microns thick, and 925 silver (800 or 835 is less reliable). REACH annex XVII compliance is mandatory in Europe.

5. **Systematically avoid**: 316L surgical steel (10 to 14% nickel despite the name), nickel silver, "silvered" jewelry, unspecified low-end white gold, thin gold plating (under 1 micron), uncertified costume jewelry.

## What to use for initial placement when allergic to nickel?

On a **fresh** piercing (less than 4 months for the lobe, up to 9 months for helix or conch), one answer only: **grade 23 ASTM F136 titanium** (Anatometal, NeoMetal, BodyArtForms, or the placement section of Nébuleuse Bijoux) or **pure niobium**. No fresh solid gold, no gold-plated silver, no steel: the wound is too sensitive. Ask your piercer to place a grade 23 titanium labret or a niobium segment ring directly.

Once healing is **confirmed** (by your piercer, never self-assessed), you can switch to healed jewelry: thick 18K gold-plated 925 silver at Nébuleuse Bijoux, solid 14K gold at Mejuri, or solid 18K gold set pieces at Maria Tash and Industrial Strength.
